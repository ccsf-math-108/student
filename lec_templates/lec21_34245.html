Example: Benford's Law¶

Benford's law, also known as the Newcomb-Benford law, the law of anomalous numbers, or the first-digit law, is an observation that in many real-life sets of numerical data, the leading digit is likely to be small. Source: Wikipedia Benford's law

Observe the distribution of the first digits of numbers according to Benford's model.

In [2]:
digits = np.arange(1, 10)
benford_model = np.log10(1 + 1/digits)
In [3]:

    'First digit', digits,
    'Benford model prob', benford_model)

Test whether or not the distribution of proportions in countries is consistent with Benford's model.

Null hypothesis: The difference in the proportions for each of the first digit values that we see is due to chance.

Alternative hypothesis: The difference in the proportions for each of the first digit values that we see is not due to chance.

Test statistic: TVD

"Bigger" values of the test statistic favor the alternative

def tvd(dist1, dist2):
    return sum(abs(dist1 - dist2)) / 2

Test whether or not there is a significant difference between the average hours of sleep for those that sleep on their side and on their back or stomach.

Null hypothesis: (The labels don't matter.) The difference in mean values is due to chance.

Alternative hypothesis: (The labels do matter.) The difference in mean values is not due to chance.

Test statistic: Absolute difference in means: abs(not_side_mean - side_mean)

Fill in the blank with "Bigger" or "Smaller":

"Bigger" values of the test statistic favor the alternative

In [79]:
def test_stat(tbl, group_label, measurement_label):
    tbl = tbl.select(measurement_label, group_label)
    grouped_tbl = tbl.group(group_label, np.average)
    averages = grouped_tbl.column(1)
    return abs(averages.item(0) - averages.item(1))
