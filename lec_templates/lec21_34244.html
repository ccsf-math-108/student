Example: Benford's Law¶

Benford's law, also known as the Newcomb-Benford law, the law of anomalous numbers, or the first-digit law, is an observation that in many real-life sets of numerical data, the leading digit is likely to be small. Source: Wikipedia Benford's law

Observe the distribution of the first digits of numbers according to Benford's model.

In [2]:
digits = np.arange(1, 10)
benford_model = np.log10(1 + 1/digits)
In [3]:
benford = Table().with_columns(
    'First digit', digits,
    'Benford model prob', benford_model)
benford.barh('First digit')

def simulate_county_first_digits():
    simulated_proporitions = sample_proportions(counties.num_rows, benford_model)
    tvd = sum(abs(simulated_proporitions - benford_model)) / 2
    return tvd

def simplify(sleep_position):
    if sleep_position == 'On your left side' or sleep_position == 'On your right side':
        return 'side'
    else:
        return 'back or stomach'

Test whether or not there is a significant difference between the average hours of sleep for those that sleep on their side and on their back or stomach.

Null hypothesis: The difference in average hours of sleep for the two sleep positions is due to chance. (aka the labels do not matter.)

Alternative hypothesis: The difference in average hours of sleep for the two sleep positions is not due to chance. (aka the labels do matter!)

Alternative hypothesis II: "Side" sleepers sleep less on average than "non-side" sleepers.

Test statistic: Absolute difference in (hours of sleep) averages for the two positions. abs(back or stomach average - side average)

Test statistic II: Absolute difference in (hours of sleep) averages for the two positions. back or stomach average - side average

def test_stat(tbl, group_label):
    grouped_tbl = tbl.group(group_label, np.average)
    avgs = grouped_tbl.column(1)
    return abs(avgs.item(0) - avgs.item(1))

Are the results significant?

Since the p-value of 9.9% is larger than the default cutoff of 5%, we fail to reject the null hypothesis. So, we continue to believe that the difference observed is due to chance. In other words there seems to be no significant difference in the average number of hours of sleep for those sleep prone (back or stomach) or in recovery (side).

Alternative hypothesis II: "Side" sleepers sleep less on average than "non-side" sleepers.
